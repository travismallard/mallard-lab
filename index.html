Who We Are

A research program in psychiatric and statistical genetics

The Mallard Lab is led by Travis T. Mallard, PhD, Assistant Professor of Psychology in the Department of Psychiatry at Massachusetts General Hospital & Harvard Medical School. The lab studies the etiology of psychopathology through the lens of genetic architecture, with particular emphasis on externalizing disorders, behavioral disinhibition, comorbidity, and heterogeneity.

Across projects, we integrate large-scale GWAS, multivariate modeling, transcriptomic and neurogenomic annotation, and imaging genetics to map shared and specific liabilities across disorders, identify developmental mechanisms, and build more interpretable tools for genomic discovery.

Active Work

Our current projects

Current projects span psychiatric genetics, developmental neurogenomics, imaging genetics, and method development, but are unified by a common aim: to explain comorbidity, pleiotropy, and heterogeneity in psychopathology with greater statistical precision and translational value.

Multivariate dissection of externalizing disorders

We are conducting large-scale multivariate genomic studies of externalizing psychopathology to resolve how liability is shared and differentiated across ADHD, conduct disorder, antisocial behavior, substance use disorders, and related dimensions of disinhibition.

Learn more →

Developmental neurogenomics of psychiatric risk

A second line of work examines how genomic liability is expressed across neurodevelopment, including projects on cortical expansion, spatiotemporal transcriptomics, and the neurodevelopmental pathways that connect genomic variation to psychiatric risk.

Learn more →

Methods for multivariate genomic discovery

The lab also develops statistical tools for complex trait genetics, including work related to Genomic SEM and new approaches designed to improve estimation, interpretability, and biologically informed inference in GWAS.

Selected Work

Publications & preprints

A selection of recent studies that reflect our broader research program in psychiatric genetics, neurodevelopmental genomics, and methodological innovation.

2026 – medRxiv
Genomic insights into substance use and disinhibitory disorders
This study analyzed data from over 4 million individuals to map the shared genetics of externalizing spectrum disorders, identifying more than 900 putative causal genes and pinpointing key synaptic pathways. In addition to generating new biological insights, we introduce a robust polygenic score that explains approximately 12% of the variance in externalizing outcomes, providing a powerful new tool for predicting psychiatric and medical risk across the human lifespan.
2025 – American Journal of Psychiatry
Characterizing the pleiotropic architecture of impulsivity and its links to psychopathology and neurodevelopment
This study analyzed genetic data from over 130,000 individuals to reveal that specific facets of impulsivity are driven by distinct neurodevelopmental pathways, arguing against the theoretical utility of treating all impulsive traits as similar. While many of these impulsive traits overlap with the broader externalizing spectrum, this study demonstrates that others, such as sensation seeking and (lack of) perseverance, have largely unique biological correlates.
2024 – Nature Mental Health
Characterizing the phenotypic and genetic structure of psychopathology in UK Biobank
This study analyzed genetic, brain imaging, and clinical data from over 400,000 individuals to map the transdiagnostic structure of ten psychiatric conditions, revealing that domain-level factors like internalizing and externalizing are significantly more informative for genomic discovery than a single general psychopathology factor. Here, we demonstrate that an all-encompassing general factor obscures unique biological correlates and environmental influences, challenging its theoretical utility for understanding psychiatric comorbidity.
