The Mallard Lab — Active Work

Research

Our work sits at the crossroads of statistical genetics, psychiatric genomics, and computational neuroscience. Across projects, we aim to explain why mental disorders so often co-occur, why they can look so different across individuals, and how genetic risk translates into biological and psychological outcomes.

Research Theme 01

Genetic architecture of externalizing disorders

Externalizing disorders — including ADHD, conduct disorder, antisocial personality disorder, and substance use disorders — are among the most heritable and most consequential conditions in psychiatry. They account for enormous individual and societal burden, yet their shared genetic foundations remain incompletely understood. A central challenge is that these disorders rarely occur in isolation: they cluster together in individuals and families, suggesting they arise from a common etiology.

Our lab uses large-scale multivariate genomic methods to characterize how genetic liability is shared and differentiated across the externalizing spectrum. Rather than studying disorders one at a time, we model their overlapping structure directly — identifying both the shared genetic architecture that explains comorbidity and the disorder-specific signals that account for why individuals with similar risk profiles nonetheless present differently.

The lab is centrally involved in the Externalizing Consortium, PsycheMERGE Network, and Psychiatric Genomics Consortium, where we lead and contribute to some of the largest genomic studies of externalizing psychopathology to date. Recently, the lab co-led the Externalizing Consortium’s flagship discovery paper, which analyzed data from more than four million individuals and generated polygenic tools with substantial predictive utility, while also yielding new hypotheses about the synaptic and neurodevelopmental pathways through which externalizing risk operates.

Research Theme 02

Developmental neurogenomics of psychiatric risk

Mental disorders are not simply adult conditions with adult causes — they emerge from developmental processes that unfold across the lifespan, and often have their roots in early neurodevelopment. Understanding when and where in the brain genetic risk manifests is important to explaining how psychiatric disorders arise and to identifying potential windows for prevention and intervention.

A second line of work in the lab examines how liability for externalizing psychopathology relates to the structure and function of the brain. This includes imaging genetics studies of cortical development and organization, as well as projects that use spatiotemporal transcriptomic data to ask when and in which cell types psychiatric risk genes are expressed across neurodevelopment. We view these as complementary approaches: imaging genetics can reveal where in the brain genetic risk manifests; neurogenomic annotation can illuminate the molecular mechanisms through which it operates.

Current projects in this area focus on the neurodevelopmental pathways linking externalizing liability to altered cortical organization and other neurodevelopmental correlates. This work draws on methods from statistical genetics, genomics, and brain imaging — areas that together can address questions that are difficult to approach from within any single discipline.

Research Theme 03

Methods for genomic discovery

Progress in psychiatric genomics depends not only on larger samples, but on better analytic tools. Standard genome-wide association studies analyze one trait at a time, which is a poor fit for a field defined by comorbidity, heterogeneity, and multidimensional outcomes. Developing methods that allow researchers to model the genetic architecture of multiple traits jointly — improving statistical power, biological interpretability, and inferential precision — is a growing area of focus for the lab.

Lab members have contributed to the development and extension of Genomic SEM (Genomic Structural Equation Modeling), a widely used framework for fitting structural models to GWAS summary statistics across multiple traits. Specific contributions include work on stratified Genomic SEM, genomic structural invariance testing, and applications of these tools to psychiatric comorbidity and neurodevelopment. The lab continues to refine and apply these approaches, and is actively working on new methods to better understand pleiotropic genetic architectures.

We are committed to open science and release software and code publicly wherever possible.
